HISTORY As a result of flooding due to rising sea levels between the 4th and 7th centuries, the inlet known as the Zwin was formed, which is referred to in the oldest historical documents as Sincfal. According to the Lex Frisionum (8th century), the southern boundary of the land of the Frisians lay by this water. It is likely that the formation of the Zwin separated Wulpen as an island from the mainland and that the area of the later island of Cadzand became flooded. The island of Wulpen was probably located to the north of the later island of Cadzand. Its exact location has not been determined. Around the year 690, Willibrord preached the gospel there and presumably went from there to Walcheren. From the beginning of the century, the archives contain the first indications of human habitation of the Flemish coastal plain. Grants of land in the coastal region to the St. Peter's Abbey in Ghent are recorded in the Liber traditionum, which dates from 941. These were primarily marshy pastures, the size of which was indicated by the number of sheep that could graze there. For example, in the year 737: a sheep pasture at Grifnigas (near St-Anna ter Muiden), which could feed thirty sheep in the summer. The records of the St. Peter's Abbey are interrupted by the arrival of the Normans. There is no information from the years between 840 and 922. During their raids along the North Sea coast around the mid-9th century, the Normans also settled on the island of Wulpen. The famous Kudrunsage recounts that King Hette fought against the robbers of Kudrun at an elk, which was called Wülpensant. It is likely that in the 9th century, to protect themselves against both the Normans and the sea, the castles Rodenburg (later called Aardenburg) and Oostburg were established.

THE ISLAND OF CADZAND The island of Cadzand is not mentioned before the year 1000. This may be related to the fact that significant properties on the island were owned by the St. Bavo's Abbey in Ghent, of which there are not many written sources from the earliest times. The name Cadesant first appears in a document concerning tithes (church taxes) of Wulpen and Cadesant, which belonged to the church in Rodenburg (Aardenburg) and had been granted to the St. Bavo's Abbey by its predecessors. Cadesant indicates that there must have already been a clear system of dikes present; the existing island was continually expanded by surrounding one piece of marshland after another with a dike and thus creating a polder. The dike construction on the land of Cadzand is among the oldest in Western Europe. In 1177, the first mention of polders on the island of Cadzand appears. Knight Walter of Axel then renounced his count's fief on Cadzand, which was spread out "super Val et super Grotenflit et in Kercpolre et Strinc et Bertenhuc et Sumpel" in favor of the St. Bavo's Abbey. A few years later, Count Philip of Alsace expanded the land holdings of St. Bavo on Cadzand by 100 measured acres. This land was located, among other places, in USudpoIre super Dondelinsvliet. One measured acre corresponded to approximately one hectare. The island of Zuidzande was formed in the same way as the island of Cadzand: an old core surrounded by a large number of small polders. At the end of the 11th century, there were at least two chapels on the island of Wulpen. These belonged to the parish of Rodenburg (Aardenburg) of the St. Bavo's Abbey.

--- IMG_6764.png ---
It is likely that around 1100 there was already a church on the site before the church was written about. The Mary Church in Cadzand is also believed to have been founded by St. Bavo, as it held the patronage rights here: that is, the right to collect tithes and appoint a pastor when a church had been established. Until 1231, pastoral care on the island of Cadzand was only exercised by one clergyman. From that year comes a request from Cadzand to the abbot of St. Bavo to appoint a chaplain in addition to the pastor. It regularly happened that the pastor, when he had left the island for one reason or another, could not make the crossing for days due to storms or rough waters, and thus no spiritual assistance could be provided. By the end of the 13th century, the island of Wulpen had four parishes, namely in Remboudsdorpe, in Brile (Avenkerke), in Westende-Wulpen (St. Precatus Church), and in Ostende-Vulpen (St. Lambertus). The islands had a regular ferry service, both with the mainland and between each other. In Cadzand, there was the "St. Mary Ferry," named after the patroness of the church of Cadzand. The names Veerhoek and Veerhoeve indicate that there were various crossing points. By the 16th century, the island of Wulpen was almost entirely swallowed by the sea, while the island of Cadzand gradually grew together with Groede and Zuidzande and by the 18th century was no longer an island.

THE MARY CHURCH IN CADZAND The Mary Church is a two-aisled hall church; the southern aisle was probably built between 1250 and 1300; the northern aisle between 1300 and 1325. The style is mainly early Gothic (Schelle Gothic), but the heavy columns are still Romanesque. The church had a high, massive tower, the so-called St. Lambertus tower, which served as a beacon for sailors. Joris Carolus mentions in his sea atlas of 1634: "When (namely at the entrance of the Wielingen) the Castle of Sluis comes to a high, clumsy tower, standing on Cadzand west of Sluis, called St. Lambert, then one has passed the French Toll and may freely proceed east towards Vlissingen." Almost nothing is known about the medieval history of the church, but it can be assumed that the building suffered greatly from the invasions of the French and English, who repeatedly came to plunder the village of Cadzand and its surroundings between 1300 and 1400, as well as from the civil wars. The political unrest in the cities and the severe floods in the 14th and 15th centuries (such as the St. Elizabeth flood of 1404 and storms of 1471, 1477, and 1497) caused great poverty in the countryside. During the civil war in Flanders in 1491, the island of Cadzand was occupied by troops of Maximilian of Austria to halt trade over the Zwin. This was accompanied by many arsons. In 1492, the peace of Cadzand was concluded.

The church also suffered greatly during the Eighty Years' War when the region was occupied by Spanish troops. The Geuzen of Vlissingen continuously conducted landings (raiding and plundering expeditions) in the Parma-occupied north of Flanders, and wherever they could, "they stole the lead from the church gutters to cast bullets for their artillery." Probably due to the significant Spanish influence in the region, the Roman Catholic faith persisted in Cadzand for a long time. In 1582, the inhabitants of Cadzand approached the Protestant classis in Breskens with a request for a pastor. After the occupation of Sluis in 1587 by Parma, all Flemish areas became "Spanish" again. In 1604, Prince Maurice conquered Philippine, IJzendijke, Aardenburg, and Sluis in one expedition, thus bringing the entire area back under the Union of Utrecht. The church of Sluis temporarily took an interest in the affairs of their co-religionists in Cadzand. The States-General decided in 1606 that a sermon should be held weekly in Cadzand from Sluis. However, the turbulent Zwin often prevented the crossing. In 1607, the church was somewhat restored, and in 1609, the first own pastor, Marcus ab Halle, accepted his service. By 1610, the southern aisle was completely restored; the northern aisle remained in ruins until 1641, when the expansion of the church became necessary. The Free of Sluis then donated a stained glass window to the church. The restoration costs were largely borne by the St. Bavo's Abbey in Ghent, which was obliged to do so according to Flemish law as the tithe collector of the parish. A remarkable situation, when the Catholic mother church pays for the restoration of a church that has converted to Protestantism.

The church of Cadzand was originally rich in land ownership; in 1610 it amounted to nearly 100 hectares; by 1665 this had shrunk to about 58 hectares. In 1677, all lands had to be sold to pay off loans and overdue interest. In 1677, the tower was in danger of completely collapsing and was demolished with the permission of the Free of Sluis. A small roof turret was placed on the church, in which the bell cast by Jan Burgerhuys in Middelburg in 1611 was hung. The sacristy, covered with a Romanesque cross vault, was rebuilt against the church, separated, and later incorporated into the rectory built in 1623, forming the central room of the pastor's residence. West Zeeuws-Vlaanderen has over the centuries received a strong influx of foreigners; it is largely a colonized land. From 1604, many refugees settled in these regions for reasons of faith. Anabaptists from Flanders, Salzburgers, but especially Huguenots and Protestants from Wallonia. After the revocation of the Edict of Nantes in 1685, Huguenots from the vicinity of Calais came to Cadzand and established a Walloon Reformed Congregation, which was significantly increased in 1713 by co-religionists from the area around Lille, while in 1771 a few more came from the regions of Picardy, Champagne, and Orleans. The Walloon Reformed Congregation held its religious services in the Reformed Church until 1719 and then received its own building. Through integration with the local population, the Walloon Congregation merged into the Reformed congregation in 1809: the light crown in the middle of the church is the only remnant of it. The presence of many French surnames in this region will always keep the history alive.

--- IMG_6768.png ---
With the Hague Treaty of 1795, State Flanders was ceded to the French Republic. In 1814, it was reunited with Holland and has since been called Zeeuws-Vlaanderen. In 1821, the entire roof of the church had to be renewed: King William I donated a subsidy of one thousand guilders. In 1894, the rectory was demolished and replaced by a new one, causing the medieval sacristy to disappear. From 1929, the church underwent significant restoration by state architect Van Heeswijk. The walls of the church were restored to their original height, and the capitals and bases of the pillars were repaired. The woodwork from the former benches dating from 1650 was used to decorate the new benches. Instead of the small roof turret, the municipal authorities built a decorative Renaissance tower against the church. During the liberation of West Zeeuws-Vlaanderen in 1944, heavy shelling caused significant damage to the church building. All the stained glass windows from the 18th century were lost. In the 1950s, the rectory was demolished, and the garden took on its current appearance. The building falls under the Foundation for Monument Care and will hopefully be preserved for a long time for future generations thanks to this foundation, the municipality of Oostburg, and the local population.
